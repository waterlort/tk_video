# Pet idea quality axes from user feedback

## Status

- Date: 2026-05-13
- Source: direct user feedback on seven pet-video ideas
- Scope: pet short-video idea generation before storyboard or prompt creation
- Use: hard creative filter for TK Content Explainer, Case Librarian, Classifier, and Judge
- Do not use as: proof that any specific topic will go viral

## User Feedback Distilled

The rejected ideas revealed the real evaluation standard:

1. A twist is weak if it is common.
   - Example failure: "pet hears can opening and thinks food is coming" is understandable but too familiar.
   - Rule: do not count a familiar pet misunderstanding as a strong contrast.

2. A twist fails if the pet must be unrealistically dumb.
   - Example failure: using a bowl as a toy is not credible if the bowl clearly looks like a bowl.
   - Rule: the animal can be instinctive, stubborn, dramatic, or confused by a plausible cue, but should not look stupid for the sake of the gag.

3. A twist fails if the object logic is visually ambiguous.
   - If the object does not look like the expected object, viewers are confused.
   - If it does look like the expected object, a contradictory interpretation may feel fake.
   - Rule: object identity must be clear, and the pet's motivation must still make sense.

4. A reversal must be strong enough.
   - Example failure: "dog blocks owner from leaving" is credible but too weak as a reversal.
   - Rule: the ending should meaningfully reframe what the viewer thought was happening.

5. Do not rely on established character knowledge.
   - Example failure: "dog performs training routine for reward" may work after an account has trained viewers, but cold viewers may not understand it.
   - Rule: a first-exposure video must explain itself through action.

6. A quirky habit is not automatically interesting.
   - Example failure: missing ritual object or hiding food can be credible but not necessarily attractive.
   - Rule: credibility is necessary, but the behavior also needs surprise, stakes, or visual consequence.

7. Strong pet content needs both contrast and motivation.
   - Contrast alone becomes fake.
   - Motivation alone becomes ordinary.
   - The target is: unexpected but believable.

8. A short pet video must not require too many sequential facts.
   - Example failure: "dog steals nail clipper, hides it, owner discovers a stash, dog repeats with brush" has a credible motive but too many beats to read quickly.
   - Rule: if the viewer must understand a chain of more than two new facts, the idea is too complex for a first-pass short video.

## New Creative Filter

Every pet idea must pass these checks before storyboard generation:

| Axis | Pass | Fail |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Freshness | The premise is not a common pet meme unless the specific behavior is extreme. | It is a familiar misunderstanding with no new visual detail. |
| Credible motive | The pet acts from instinct, fear, comfort, avoidance, curiosity, resource guarding, social attachment, play, or sensory trigger. | The pet behaves like a tiny human employee, judge, or planner without evidence. |
| Visual proof | The first 2 seconds show the behavior clearly. | The viewer needs a long caption or prior account lore. |
| Contrast strength | The payoff changes the viewer's interpretation. | The payoff only adds a small extra detail. |
| Cold-viewer clarity | A new viewer can understand it immediately. | It requires knowing the pet's recurring routine. |
| Animal respect | The pet is funny because of real behavior, not because it is written as foolish. | The joke depends on the pet being implausibly dumb. |
| Consequence | The action creates visible chaos, obstacle, physical mismatch, failure, or high-stakes reaction. | The habit is merely cute or mildly odd. |
| Information density | One setup, one action, one payoff. Every second has visible new information, but the viewer does not need to remember a chain. | The idea needs multiple props, hidden backstory, repeated discoveries, or a long cause-effect explanation. |

## Preferred Motive Families

Use these first:

- sensory startle or activation;
- care/grooming avoidance;
- physical mismatch between body and task;
- being caught in the act with visible evidence;
- stubborn refusal with physical consequence;
- social attachment that creates a visible obstacle;
- curiosity that triggers an unexpected chain reaction.

Use cautiously:

- food sound confusion;
- jealousy over another pet;
- owner leaving;
- pet rituals;
- button/talking-dog setups;
- voiceover-only personality.

Usually reject:

- pet as quality inspector, manager, judge, or worker unless the behavior itself proves it;
- object confusion that viewers cannot read;
- weak reversals with no visible consequence;
- common pet jokes without a fresh mechanism;
- multi-step plans where the pet steals/hides/reveals/repeats several objects before the joke lands.

## Idea Generation Rule

Future pet ideas should be written as:

`viewer assumption -> visible pet action -> credible motive revealed -> consequence/payoff`

Not:

`cute pet -> caption explains a human-like role -> pet makes a face`

## Complexity Budget

For a first-exposure 6-10 second pet idea:

- max one pet;
- max one main prop;
- max one location change;
- max one reversal;
- max two facts the viewer must track;
- payoff should be visible as an action or physical consequence, not a discovered explanation.
